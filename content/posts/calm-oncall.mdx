On-call isn't supposed to ruin your weekend. After a few rotations where it did, I collected the handful of habits that actually moved the needle.

## 1. Write the runbook before the fire

Every alert I own has a runbook with three sections: **what fired**, **what to check first**, **how to resolve**. Ten minutes of writing today saves thirty minutes of 3am tab-switching later. The runbook lives one link away from the alert.

## 2. Kill flappy alerts the morning after

Any alert that paged me and resolved itself without my action gets ruthlessly re-evaluated the next morning. Either the threshold is wrong, the window is too short, or the thing is genuinely noisy and needs a different signal. I don't let "it's mostly fine" alerts live in my rotation.

## 3. Post-incident review, even for the small ones

Not the theater version. The five-line version:

- What happened?
- Why didn't we catch it earlier?
- What's the smallest change that would've prevented it?
- Who's doing it by when?
- Anything to add to the runbook?

That's it. No blame, no multi-page doc. Just a crisp thread the team can skim.

---

None of this is clever. It just compounds. Six months of doing these three things turned on-call from "brace for impact" into "probably nothing, but I'm ready."
